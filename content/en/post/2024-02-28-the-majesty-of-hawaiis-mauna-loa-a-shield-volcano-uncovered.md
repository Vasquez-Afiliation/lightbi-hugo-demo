## Mauna Loa's Geological Formation

Mauna Loa's geological formation began approximately between 600,000 and 1 million years ago, characterized by the gradual accumulation of basaltic lava flows that shaped its immense structure. This shield volcano, with its low viscosity basaltic lava, spans over 1,900 square miles, establishing itself as the largest active volcano on Earth. The continuous volcanic activity of Mauna Loa has resulted in 33 eruptions since 1843, highlighting its dynamic [nature](https://magmamatters.com/the-art-and-science-of-volcano-monitoring/ "The Art and Science of Volcano Monitoring").

The basaltic lava flows on Mauna Loa contribute to its shield volcano classification. Shield volcanoes are known for their broad, gently sloping profiles, which are created by the runny nature of the basaltic lava. This lava type allows for the formation of extensive lava fields, building up the volcano's massive size over time. The geological formation of Mauna Loa showcases the power and persistence of volcanic activity, shaping the landscape of Hawaii.

Over the millennia, Mauna Loa's volcanic activity has played a crucial role in forming the unique topography of the region. The volcano's eruptions haven't only contributed to its impressive size but have also influenced the surrounding environment, creating a habitat that supports diverse flora and fauna. Studying Mauna Loa's geological history provides valuable insights into the Earth's dynamic processes and the impact of volcanic activity on shaping our planet's surface.

## Noteworthy Eruptions of Mauna Loa

The historical record of Mauna Loa reveals a series of noteworthy eruptions that have significantly impacted Hawaii's landscape and history. One of the most recent significant eruptions occurred in 1984, drawing global attention for three weeks. This eruption came perilously close, just 4 miles away, from the town of Hilo, vividly demonstrating the sheer power of Mauna Loa.

In a bold attempt to redirect the lava flow away from Hilo during a threatening eruption in 1935, aerial bombing was carried out on the slopes of Mauna Loa. This event highlights the innovative measures taken to mitigate the destructive potential of the volcano. Subsequently, in 1950, Mauna Loa unleashed vast lava flows that swiftly reached the sea, causing widespread impact on the surrounding areas.

The 1868 eruption of Mauna Loa stands out as one of the deadliest disasters in Hawaiian history, leaving a somber reminder of the volcano's unpredictable nature. These eruptions, varying in scale and intensity, underscore the continuous geological activity of Mauna Loa and its enduring influence on the Hawaiian archipelago.
