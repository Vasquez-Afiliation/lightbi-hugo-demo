## Types of Eruptive Patterns

Composite volcanoes exhibit various types of eruptive patterns, showcasing a range of explosive events and lava output rates. One common eruptive pattern observed at composite volcanoes is the occurrence of Strombolian explosions. These explosions are characterized by the ejection of incandescent cinder, lapilli, and lava bombs, accompanied by gas and ash emissions. Strombolian explosions are frequent and contribute to the continuous activity seen at many composite volcanoes.

In addition to Strombolian explosions, major explosive events can occur around twice a year at composite volcanoes. These events represent more intense eruptions that can result in significant ash plumes and pyroclastic flows. Furthermore, paroxysms, which are rare but powerful explosive events involving multiple vents, can also occur at composite volcanoes, showcasing the unpredictable nature of these volcanic systems.

Effusive eruptions, on the other hand, are less common at composite volcanoes, with occurrences approximately every 35 years. These eruptions are characterized by varying lava output rates and can lead to the formation of lava flows. Prior to effusive eruptions, phenomena such as landslides, crater collapses, and tsunamis may occur, indicating the complex interplay of [geological processes leading to volcanic activity at composite volcanoes](https://magmamatters.com/geothermal-energy-and-its-volcanic-origins/ "Geothermal Energy and Its Volcanic Origins") like Stromboli.

## Factors Influencing Eruptions

Factors influencing eruptions on composite volcanoes encompass a range of geological characteristics, including magma composition and eruption types.

- **Magma Composition:**

The composition of magma in composite volcanoes, often andesitic or dacitic, significantly influences the explosiveness of eruptions. These magmas tend to be viscous, leading to explosive eruptions as gases struggle to escape.

- **Type of Eruption:**

Composite volcanoes exhibit a spectrum of eruption types, from phreatic eruptions driven by groundwater to magmatic eruptions fueled by molten rock. Vulcanian, Sub-Plinian, and Plinian eruptions are commonly observed.

- **Effusive to Explosive:**

Eruptions on composite volcanoes can vary from effusive, where lava flows steadily, to explosive, characterized by ash clouds, pyroclastic flows, and lahars. The transition between these states can be rapid and unpredictable.

- **Caldera-Forming Eruptions:**

Larger caldera-forming eruptions have the potential to reshape the landscape dramatically. These catastrophic events can lead to the collapse of the volcanic edifice, forming a caldera and impacting future eruptive patterns.

- **Subduction Zones:**

Composite volcanoes typically form above subduction zones, such as the Pacific Ring of Fire. The interaction of tectonic plates in these regions influences magma composition, water content, and the overall eruptive behavior of the volcanoes.

## Hazards Associated With Eruptions

The hazards associated with eruptions from composite volcanoes encompass a variety of risks to both human populations and the environment.

Pyroclastic flows, which are fast-moving currents of hot gas and volcanic matter, pose a significant danger to communities living near these volcanoes. These flows can travel at high speeds, reaching temperatures over 1,000 degrees Celsius, destroying everything in their path.

Lahars, another hazardous phenomenon, are mudflows generated by the mixing of volcanic debris with water, often triggered by heavy rainfall or melting snow on the volcano. These lahars can travel long distances, carrying debris and posing a threat to infrastructure and lives downstream.

During eruptions, composite [volcanoes also release hazardous gases such as sulfur dioxide](https://magmamatters.com/the-art-and-science-of-volcano-monitoring/ "The Art and Science of Volcano Monitoring") and carbon dioxide. These gases can impact air quality in the vicinity of the volcano, causing respiratory issues for humans and animals. Additionally, volcanic ash clouds produced during eruptions can disrupt air travel, affecting flight paths and airport operations. The ash can damage aircraft engines and pose risks to aviation safety.

Furthermore, the ejection of volcanic bombs and projectiles during explosive eruptions can endanger lives and property near the volcano's summit. These large rocks can be thrown great distances, causing destruction and adding to the overall hazards associated with composite volcano eruptions.
